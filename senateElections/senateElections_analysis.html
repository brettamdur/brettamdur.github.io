Senate Election Competitiveness Since the 70s: The More Things Change . . .

The rise in political partisanship hasn't made Senate elections much less competitive. But it might be making them much less volatile.

One hot toy this holiday season is the Level Up Gaming Chair. It's a swivel seat with an integrated tablet computer, complete with joystick and headphones. FOR TWO YEAR OLDS. Or, more accurately, for parents who need to "level up" their toddler. Really.

In 1975, the hot holiday toy was a Pet Rock. Really.

As we were poking around with data this election season, we thought about how different our world is now from what it was then. It got us to wondering: have political elections, like holiday toy crazes, become unrecognizable from what they were in the mid 70s? We thought one way to help explore this question would be to examine Senate elections since 1976 to see if we could spot any interesting changes in how competitive they are. Why did we start with 1976? Because that's the earliest year for which the Federal Election Commission makes data available on its website. And because nobody could argue that the world hasn't changed much since then. Cause, you know . . . Pet Rocks.

We were especially interested in the question of whether the dramatic increase in political polarization over the years has caused a decrease in the competitiveness of Senate elections. For example, it's well known that Americans are increasingly clustering in counties that are politically homogenous: the number of Americans who live in a county that favored one party's presidential candidate by more than 20 percentage points has increased steadily from 26% in 1976 to 58.2% in 2020. Do these and other similar dynamics that drive increased polarization also reduce the competitiveness of statewide Senate elections?

The answer is interesting. On one hand, Senate elections aren't becoming appreciably less compentitive. They seem resiliant to the polarization forces that one might think would push races to become less close. On the other hand, though, Senate election results are becoming decidedly less variable from year to year, a phenomenon that may in fact be attributable to a more polarized electorate.

We dig into the data below, and we point out some things we discovered along the way that we thought were interesting. We also encourage you to check out "How Competitive Are Senate Elections in Your State?", which is a companion page to this piece where you can use interactive tools to explore the data on a state-by-state basis.

Yearly Winner Margins

Let's start with the big picture. Our first chart shows that the average margin of victory in Senate elections isn't really trending in any particular direction. The average victory margin across the entire period from 1976 to 2022 was 21 points. The average between 1976 and 1994 (19.66 points) was only slightly below the average for the whole period, and the average from 1998 to 2022 (22.29 points) was only slightly above the average for the whole period.

The interesting trend that jumps out from this chart isn't around a change in average competitiveness, but rather a change in how variable competitiveness has become. As you can see, Senate election competitiveness was a lot less stable in the first period than it was in the second, seesawing more wildly from year to year before 1998, while showing much less volitility from 1988 onward. Why the change? One possibility is increased polarization, which many say began its big climb in the mid-90s. Because polarization, by definition, makes voters more set in their party affiliations, it would make sense that it would cause swings in competitiveness within a state to become less dramatic. The law of large numbers (365 and 446 elections in the pre and post 1998 periods, respectively) gets us to roughly the same average competitiveness across both periods, but less voter openness to party switching means less variability in competitiveness since the 90s.

A look at the state-by-state analysis in our companion piece "How Competitive Are Senate Elections in Your State?" provides some good corraboration for this theory. Hawaii and Idaho (see chart) are about as far apart as states can get politically (even though they're right next to each other in alphabetical state order -- it must be so awkward when they sit at their desks every morning in homeroom). You can see that even within individual states with similar average competitiveness, the variability in competitiveness decreases over time, as the elections get closer to the mean (represented by the horizontal line). Go to the companion piece and check out the other states to see a remarkably similar pattern across many (although of course not all) states.

We'll save you some time: 36 states show decreasing variabilty in competitiveness over time, while only 14 states show increasing variability. That's true even though there's an almost even split between states where competition is increasing since 1976 (26) and states where competition is decreaing (24). Bottom line: there's a strong argument to be made that while increasing polarization hasn't made Senate elections less competitive, it has made them less volatile.

What About Incumbents?

You might be able to make a decent argument that the the benefits of incumbency are declining as the importance of party loyalty increases.

First, some background. As you probably know, incubency is a huge advantage in elections. In the 446 Senate elections since 1982 where an incumbent was running, the incubment has won a whopping 91 percent of the time. So if incumbency is so determinative, the winner's margin must be really high in elections where an incumbent runs compared to elections where an incumbent doesn't run. Right?

Actually, not so much. The chart below compares the average winner margin for elections where an incumbent ran and where an incumbent didn't run (it also excludes the few elections where the winner ran unopposed). Since 1982 there's only about a six point difference between the average margin in elections with incumbents (23.38 point margin) compared to elections without them (17.14 point margin). Six points certainly isn't nothing, but it's less than a third of the average margin in all Senate elections since 1982. As you can see from the chart, there's actully been a really large (greater than 10 points, let's say) difference between incumbent and non-incumbent elections in only five of the 21 Senate election years since 1982.

Average Senate Election Winner Margin: 1982-2022

The next chart uses the same data but displays it in a different way. Here, for each year, we're looking at the difference between the average margin in elections with incumbents and elections without incumbents. In other words, for each year we're showing the gap between the two lines shown in the previous chart. Returning to our original point about the possibly diminishing importance of incumbency, you can see that since 2008, the incumbency advantage has been less dramatic, at least as compared to the period between 1992 and 2006. In fact, in half of the election years after 2006, the average margin in elections without an incumbent was bigger than the average margin in elections with an incumbent (we'll leave for another day an assessment of what was going on between 1982 and 1990, when four out of five election years saw bigger margins in elections where incumbents didn't run).

Incumbents vs. Non-Incumbents

So if the incumbency advantage is diminishing, why? It may just be that party loyalty trumps all these days. Yes, incumbents are likely to win, but not because they generate landslides. More and more, incumbents winning general elections might be more linked to the incumbent being affiliated with the party that's more popular in their state than it does with incumbency itself. In other words, the fact that for the most part margins in non-incumbent elections are becoming more similar to the margins in incumbent elections suggests that the margins in all elections are driven more by the popularity of the parties in each state than by any incumbency advantage.

More support for this line of thinking comes from the connection between the results of Senate and Presidential elections within a state. In the just completed 2022 Senate elections, only one of the 35 Senate elections resulted in a winner from a different party than the winner of the Presidential election in that state (Republican Ron Johnson won in Wisconsin, where President Biden won in 2020). That connection has been stronger than ever over the last 10 years -- the same period during which the margin difference in incumbent and non-incumbent races has compressed.

More Fun With Competitiveness

Quick: what state has had the tightest Senate elections since 1976? If your mind is cycling through the infamous swing states to find an answer, don't bother. It's actually (drum roll) . . . North Carolina. The least competitive state is less surprising: Hawaii. Things don't move much even if you only look at elections since 2000: North Carolina still takes the top spot, with Wyoming becoming least competitive and Hawaii second.

There's lots more Senate election data to explore. Have your own fun with it, and let us know what you find.
