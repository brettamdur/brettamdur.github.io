People, Parties and Pet Rocks: Senate Election Competitiveness Since 1976

The importance of party over candidate is growing. But maybe not in the way you'd expect.

Poking around with data this election season while doing holiday shopping meant we couldn't help thinking about how different our political world is now from what it was then. In politics, the biggest difference might be the well documented -- and deeply felt -- rise in party partisanship. It got us to wondering: how has this rise in partisanship impacted election results? More specifically, how does polarization affect how close elections are?

We thought one way to explore this question would be to examine Senate elections since 1976 to see if we could spot any interesting changes in competitiveness. Why did we start with 1976? Because that's the earliest year for which the Federal Election Commission makes data available on its website. And because nobody could argue that the world hasn't changed much since then. Cause, you know . . . Pet Rocks.

Here's the interesting answer: The average competitiveness of Senate elections hasn't changed much over the years. What has changed, though, is the variability in competitiveness from year to year. It's decreasing. And there are some good arguments for why polarization is causing the reduced volatility. Let's take a look.

Partisanship's Impact On Competitiveness

You might think that rising partisanship would cause Senate elections to become less competitive. Rising polarization, by definition, means that voters become more set in their party affiliations. Shouldn't this in turn cause elections to become less competitive as red states get redder and blue states get bluer? For example, it's well known that Americans are increasingly clustering in counties that are politically homogenous: the number of Americans who live in a county that favored one party's presidential candidate by more than 20 percentage points has increased steadily from 26% in 1976 to 58.2% in 2020. Are similar dynamics at the state level causing a drop in Senate election competitiveness?

In a word, no. As you can see from the chart below, the average margin of victory in Senate elections actually hasn't changed much over the years. The average victory margin across the entire period from 1976 to 2022 was 21 points. The average between 1976 and 1994 (19.66 points) was only slightly below the average for the whole period, and the average from 1998 to 2022 (22.29 points) was only slightly above the average for the whole period.

So what's going on? One explanation might come from what we could call The Herschel Walker Effect. This year's Senate election in Georgia showed us that a candidate with flaws as deeply profound as Walker's can actually come awfully close (less than three percentage points) to becoming a US Senator. It's impossible to precisely quantify the percentage point impact that those "deeply profound flaws" had on Walker's election results. What's not hard to see, though, is that in a world without strong party loyalty, Walker would have lost by a much larger margin (yes, the state is changing, but Georgia has clearly been very red: Republicans had, in fact, won seven of the eight Senate elections in Georgia prior to Walker's by an average of almost 10 points). So sure, partisanship can make elections less competitive than they otherwise would be in states where one party has a clear partisan advantage and where other factors -- like candidate quality -- are equal. But, on the flip side of the competitiveness coin, Walker shows us that partisanship can also make elections more competitive than they otherwise would be. That happens in elections where voter loyalty to a candidate's party counteracts the other candidate's advantage in suitability for office.

Partisanship's Real Impact

You can see from just a glance at the previous chart that the interesting trend isn't about a change in average competitiveness. It's about a change in how variable competitiveness has become. That is, as you can see from that chart, Senate election competitiveness is less variable than it once was*. You can see that it was a lot less stable in the first period than it was in the second, seesawing more wildly from year to year before 1998, while showing less volatility from 1988 onward.

This next chart shows the change in competitiveness variability over time, by state. For each state, we calculated the average competitiveness (meaning the average winner margin) across all the Senate elections during the period. We then looked at how much the winner margin in each election deviated from that average (what statisticians would call "the residual"). The line shown here shows the trend in deviation (which you can think of as "variability" or "volatility") in election results from the average for each state. A downward slope from left to right means volatility is decreasing, while an upward slope means volatility is increasing.

As you can see, by an almost a 4:1 margin, far more states are showing a trend over time toward less volatility versus more volatility in the competitiveness of their results.

Trends in Competitiveness Variability in US Senate Elections: 1976-2022

So there's been a change around how much things change: they used to change more than they do now. Why? One possibility is increased polarization, which many say began its big climb in the mid-90s, right around when the competitiveness variability shown in these results started decreasing. Because polarization makes voters more set in their party affiliations, it would make sense that it would cause swings in competitiveness within a state to become less dramatic. The law of large numbers (365 and 446 elections in the pre and post 1998 periods, respectively) gets us to roughly the same average competitiveness across both periods, but less voter openness to party switching means less variability in competitiveness since the 90s. Stated another way, it's not that partisanship is making red states redder and blue states bluer. It's that every state is becoming more reliably as red or as blue as it's generally been.

You can look at the state-by-state analysis in our companion piece "How Competitive Are Senate Elections in Your State?" to see this in action. Turn on the "Winner Margin: State" line to show the average competitiveness in each state. Then take a look at the pattern of dots, each one representing a single election, in the states. You'll see that most often, the dots cluster closer to the line to the right of the chart, meaning in years closer to today. By way of example, Hawaii and Idaho (see chart) are about as far apart as states can get politically (even though they're right next to each other in alphabetical state order -- it must be so awkward when they sit at their desks every morning in homeroom). You can see that even within individual states with similar average competitiveness, the variability in competitiveness decreases over time, as the elections get closer to the mean (represented by the horizontal line). Go to the companion piece and check out the other states to see a remarkably similar pattern across many (although of course not all) states.

This change in variability has happened even though there's an almost dead even split between states where competitiveness is increasing since 1976 (26) and states where competition is decreasing (24) (turn on the "Trend: State" line in the companion piece to see that). Bottom line: there's a strong argument to be made that while increasing polarization hasn't made Senate elections less competitive, it has made them less volatile.

What About Incumbents?

"But wait!" you say, being the savvy political observer that you are. "What about the incumbency advantage? I happen to know that in the 446 Senate elections since 1982 where an incumbent was running, the incumbent has won a whopping 91 percent of the time! So if incumbency is so determinative, the winner's margin must be really high in elections where an incumbent runs compared to elections where an incumbent doesn't run. So things must look different if we account for incumbency, right?"

Slow your roll there, smartypants.

Incumbency doesn't affect the analysis as much as you might expect. The chart below compares the average winner margin for elections where an incumbent ran and where an incumbent didn't run (it also excludes the few elections where the winner ran unopposed). Since 1982 there's only about a six point difference between the average margin in elections with incumbents (23.38 point margin) compared to elections without them (17.14 point margin). Six points certainly isn't nothing, but it's less than a third of the average margin in all Senate elections since 1982. As you can see from the chart, there's actually been a really large (greater than 10 points, let's say) difference between incumbent and non-incumbent elections in only five of the 21 Senate election years since 1982.

Average Senate Election Winner Margin: 1982-2022

The next chart uses the same data as the previous one but displays it in a different way. Here, for each year, we're looking at the difference between the average margin in elections with incumbents and elections without incumbents. In other words, for each year we're showing the gap between the two lines shown in the previous chart. You can see that since 2008, the incumbency advantage has been less dramatic, at least as compared to the period between 1992 and 2006. In fact, in half of the election years after 2006, the average margin in elections without an incumbent was bigger than the average margin in elections with an incumbent (we'll leave for another day an assessment of what was going on between 1982 and 1990, when four out of five election years saw bigger margins in elections where incumbents didn't run).

Incumbents vs. Non-Incumbents

Why doesn't incumbency have more of an impact on winner margin, and why does the incumbency advantage appear to be shrinking somewhat? Once again, it may just be that party loyalty trumps everything these days. Incumbents win now with the same frequency that they always did. Increasingly, though, incumbents winning general elections might be more linked to the incumbent being affiliated with the party that rules their state than it is with incumbency itself. In other words, the fact that for the most part margins in non-incumbent elections are becoming more similar to the margins in incumbent elections might suggest that the margins in all elections are driven more by the popularity of the parties in each state than by any incumbency advantage.

More support for this line of thinking comes from the connection between the results of Senate and Presidential elections within a state. In the just completed 2022 Senate elections, only one of the 35 Senate elections resulted in a winner from a different party than the winner of the Presidential election in that state (Republican Ron Johnson won in Wisconsin, where President Biden won in 2020). That connection has been stronger than ever over the last 10 years -- roughly the same period during which the margin difference in incumbent and non-incumbent races has compressed.

More Fun With Competitiveness

Quick: what state has had the tightest Senate elections since 1976? If your mind is cycling through the infamous swing states to find an answer, don't bother. It's actually (drum roll) . . . North Carolina. The least competitive state is less surprising: Hawaii. Things don't move much even if you only look at elections since 2000: North Carolina still takes the top spot, with Wyoming becoming least competitive and Hawaii second.

There's lots more Senate election data to explore. Have your own fun with it, and let us know what you find.

*Note For Nerds: Running a Levene test on the significance of the difference in the yearly mean variance in the elections between 1976 and 1996 and those from 1998 to today generates a p-value of 0.18. To oversimplify things somewhat, this means that there is an 18 percent chance that the decrease in the variance that we discuss in this article is just due to chance. That's a lot bigger than, say, the 5% threshold that's often used as the maximum "likelihood that it's attributable to chance" number in other contexts (things like medical or scientific research). It's also true, of course, that our sample sizes are very small here (11 years in the first period, and 13 in the second). So while it's certainly possible that the trend toward declining volatility is less real than it might appear, we think an 82 percent chance that the difference isn't just due to chance is enough to justify the kind of thinking about the issue that we're doing here.
