The Electoral College Doesn't Favor Red States The Way You Think It Does

If you think the candidate with the most votes should win, then sure, the Electoral College is unfair. But if you also think that the Electoral College gives red states a big advantage, read on.

Background

You probably know that because of our Electoral College system, the winner of a US Presidential election isn't always the candidate who receives the most votes. In fact, five times in US history, the candidate who won the Electoral College vote actually lost the popular vote (which is a straight count of how many people voted for each candidate). This has now happened twice in the last six elections: once in 2000 (Bush defeated Gore but lost the popular vote 48.4% to 47.9%) and once in 2016 (Trump defeated Clinton but lost the popular vote 48.2% to 46.1%).

If you think this is a problem, blame the inner workings of the Electoral College. That's because the number of EC votes that the Constitution allocates to each state is equal to the number of the state's Congressional representatives, House and Senate combined. House representation, of course, is based on population. But since every state, regardless of population, gets exactly two senators, states' total EC votes aren't proportional to their population. This means that smaller states get EC votes that are out of whack with -- meaning greater than -- the number of EC votes the state would get if EC votes were distributed proportionally according to the states' actual populations.

The result is that voters in low population states have more potential impact than voters in high population states. Let's look at the two extremes. Wyoming has about 575 thousand people and gets three Electoral College votes. So in Wyoming, each Electoral College vote represents about 192 thousand Wyoming people. But California has about 40 million people and 55 Electoral College votes, meaning each Electoral College vote represents 727 thousand people. The Myth of the Red State Advantage

It's easy to think that red states are rural and less populated, so that their voters must have a lot undue influence on electoral college results in presidential elections. But it's not that simple. Let's take a look.

We'll use a measure that's been called "voting power". This is the electoral votes per voter within a state, meaning the number of electoral votes at stake in a state divided by the number of the state's eligible voters. In fact, looking at 2016 presidential election results, if you divide the total population of states that voted Republican by the total number of EC votes they represent, you see that each 1 million voters in those states is associated with 1.59 EV votes. That same calculation for states that voted Democratic? It's 1.57. So red state voters do not have the huge electoral college advantage that many people think they do. So if R's don't really have a big EC advantage, how did they win 2016 while losing the popular vote?
